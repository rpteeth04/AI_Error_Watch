✦ Project Methodology & Workflow

Objective

To develop a risk-based quality oversight framework (AI-CoQ) that systematically categorizes failure modes in Generative AI when drafting Adverse Event (AE) narratives, ensuring strict compliance with ICH-GCP E6(R3) principles of human-led clinical accountability.

Core Methodology

This project utilizes a simulation-based stress-testing methodology. Rather than logging superficial syntax errors, the framework evaluates the architectural limitations of Large Language Models (LLMs) when processing synthetic, high-complexity clinical scenarios.

The resulting taxonomy forces clinical reviewers to audit against known structural "blind spots"—Processing Drifts, Elicitation Gaps, and Translation Gaps.

Execution Workflow

1

Synthetic Data Generation

Design of complex virtual patient profiles, incorporating masked confounders and colloquial symptom reporting.

2

GenAI Drafting Simulation

Feeding raw, unstructured source notes into a GenAI model to produce initial drafts of structured Adverse Event narratives.

3

Taxonomic Audit

Applying the 3-tier failure mode taxonomy to systematically identify processing errors, elicitation gaps, and mistranslations.

4

Human Countersignature

The investigator acts as the final arbiter, correcting architectural omissions and formally signing off.

Direct ICH-GCP E6(R3) Regulatory Mapping

This framework is heavily rooted in the modernized principles of ICH-GCP E6(R3), addressing the introduction of computerized systems into the data lifecycle.

❖ Annex 1 & Principle 2.12

The investigator retains ultimate responsibility for the trial and safety reporting, regardless of the AI drafting tools employed. Human-in-the-loop is mandatory.

❖ Section 4.9 (Records)

Adherence to ALCOA++ principles. The AI's tendency for "Factual Drift" directly violates the Accurate and Original requirements of clinical source data.

AI Error Watch: A Failure Mode Taxonomy

Evaluating Generative AI-Drafted Adverse Event Narratives under ICH-GCP E6(R3)[1]

Human-in-the-Loop
AI never holds final authority over a safety-relevant record.

The Problem

When GenAI tools draft narratives from remote or virtual patient data, failures aren't always obvious on a quick read-through.

The Mandate

"Reviewers shouldn't just re-read and hope something looks off. They must actively audit against known architectural limitations."

Key Guardrail

The AI-drafted narrative must be reviewed and countersigned by the investigator before entering the Trial Master File (TMF)[2]. Human sign-off is a hard clinical requirement, not a formality.

Category 1: Processing Errors

The AI has access to the relevant data, but handles it incorrectly.

A. Factual Drift

Def: Quantitative data altered or misstated.

Ex: Source notes report 101.2°F; AI writes "high-grade fever of 103°F".

➔ Action: Cross-check every quantitative value against source records.

B. Omission

Def: Crucial source data left out.

Ex: Concomitant OTC painkiller use is excluded.

➔ Action: Confirm all co-morbidities and confounders transfer over.

C. Invented Interpretation

Def: Unwarranted clinical judgments/causality inferred by AI.

Ex: AI writes "likely related to study drug" from raw symptom notes.

➔ Action: Verify all causality judgments come strictly from the investigator.

D. False Confidence

Def: Patient uncertainty smoothed into definitive facts.

Ex: "Kind of dizzy, not sure" becomes "Patient experienced dizziness".

➔ Action: Ensure hedged or uncertain patient language is preserved.

Category 2: Elicitation Gaps

The data was never captured because AI lacks rapport-building capabilities.

Non-Disclosure

Definition Patient withholds sensitive data that an experienced clinician could draw out.

Example: Patient hides smoking history; clinician would have noticed signs and probed.

Reviewer Action

For virtual visits, confirm known confounder categories were actively probed, not just passively awaited.

Passive Capture

Definition Patient doesn't know a sign is relevant, and AI cannot investigate beyond stated inputs.

Example: Patient reports limited mouth opening; underlying swollen lymph nodes go undetected.

Reviewer Action

Confirm the review step includes prompts for differential-diagnosis-driven follow-up.

Category 3: Translation Gaps

Data is captured, but its clinical meaning is lost in translation.

Colloquial-to-Clinical Mistranslation

Definition

Regional, cultural, or informal patient language is converted too literally or inaccurately into clinical terms.

Illustrative Example

A patient describes a "burning" sensation used loosely to mean different things regionally; the AI selects the wrong clinical equivalent.

Investigator Check

Confirm symptom descriptions align with locally and culturally informed clinical interpretations, especially in multilingual or multi-site trial populations.

The Structural Reality

Ethical & Equitable GenAI Deployment

Human Accountability [1, 2]

Per ICH-GCP E6(R3) and FDA guidelines, AI cannot hold clinical accountability. The investigator retains ultimate responsibility for all safety reporting, making "human-in-the-loop" a legal mandate.

Data Privacy & PHI [4, 6]

GenAI models used in drafting must operate in zero-trust, HIPAA/GDPR-compliant environments. Any leakage of Protected Health Information (PHI) into public training weights is a catastrophic violation.

Equity & Bias [4, 5]

Pre-trained models can perpetuate structural biases, especially regarding underrepresented populations. Active, culturally-competent human oversight is required to ensure equitable safety monitoring.
